Design direction

Visual and typography changes

Typography
Reduce font mixing
The site mixes serif italic, monospace, and sans-serif in the first three scrolls. For a CMO audience: use sans-serif only for all body copy and headings. Reserve monospace exclusively for the "For the technically curious" section (§ 03 equivalent). Remove italic serif emphasis except in the manifesto pull-quote. Result: cleaner, more professional, easier to scan.
Code blocks
One code block, one location
Currently five distinct code/terminal elements appear in the first half of the page. Reduce to one: a short, simplified YAML block in the "For the technically curious" section only. Remove the animated agent.fetch() terminal box from the hero. Remove the full 3,000-word prompt from the homepage. Remove the full file tree from the homepage — keep in /docs.
Section labels
Keep § numbers, humanise labels
The § notation gives the site its editorial character — preserve it. Change the labels to be conversational: "§ 01 · Why it matters" (not "Thesis"), "§ 02 · Where your brand needs to live" (not "Three surfaces"), "§ 03 · How it's built" (not "The protocol"). The CMO reads these as signposts, not chapter headings.
Whitespace
More breathing room between sections
Increase vertical padding between sections — particularly around the hero, the pricing cards, and the manifesto section. Each section should feel like a complete, self-contained thought, not part of a continuous wall of content. The manifesto section especially needs space above and below to land with impact.
Hero
Text-only hero — no code
After removing the code boxes, the hero is headline, standfirst, two CTAs, and the manifesto pull-quote. This is cleaner and more confident. The temptation will be to fill the space with something — resist it. Whitespace in the hero signals confidence. The copy is strong enough to stand alone.
Pricing cards
Clear visual hierarchy
The Expert Session card should be visually elevated — a subtle green border accent (not a different background colour, just a border) and a "Most popular" badge. The Free card should feel genuinely accessible, not like a downgrade — same card style, same visual weight. The Sprint card should feel premium, not intimidating.
